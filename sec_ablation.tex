\section{Ablation Studies}
\label{sec:ablation}

We isolate the contribution of each design decision through four ablation experiments.

\subsection{Effect of Hybrid Decoupling}

Table~\ref{tab:naive_vs_hybrid} compares na\"ive iterative refinement (feeding the full conditioned output back without decoupling) against our hybrid approach.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Na\"ive vs.\ hybrid iterative refinement. Na\"ive iteration improves rotation at the cost of translation; hybrid fusion achieves superior rotation improvement while preserving translation accuracy.}
\label{tab:naive_vs_hybrid}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lcccc@{}}
\toprule
Method & ATE$\downarrow$ & ARE$\downarrow$ & $\Delta$ATE & $\Delta$ARE \\
\midrule
Unconditioned baseline & 0.161 & 4.39\degree{} & --- & --- \\
Na\"ive self-refine (iter 5) & 0.195 & 3.60\degree{} & +21\% & $-$18\% \\
Hybrid MC (iter 2) & 0.160 & 3.13\degree{} & $-$1\% & $-$29\% \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Na\"ive iteration degrades ATE by 21\% while improving ARE by only 18\%. In contrast, hybrid fusion achieves 29\% ARE improvement with negligible translation change. This confirms the theoretical prediction of Section~\ref{sec:theory}: translation does not contract under self-conditioning and must be anchored to the unconditioned estimate.

\subsection{Sensitivity to Conditioning Quality}
\label{sec:noise_sensitivity}

To characterise the basin of attraction, we condition Pi3X with ground truth poses corrupted by varying levels of Gaussian noise (Table~\ref{tab:noise_robustness}).

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Effect of conditioning pose quality on output accuracy. Translation noise is isotropic Gaussian in scene coordinates. Beyond $\sim$10\degree{} rotation noise, conditioning becomes counterproductive and the model diverges.}
\label{tab:noise_robustness}
\begin{tabular}{@{}cccc@{}}
\toprule
Rot.\ noise (\degree{}) & Trans.\ noise & ATE$\downarrow$ & ARE$\downarrow$ \\
\midrule
0 (perfect GT) & 0 & 0.097 & 1.22\degree{} \\
1 & 0.05 & 0.087 & 1.30\degree{} \\
2 & 0.10 & 0.080 & 1.60\degree{} \\
5 & 0.20 & 0.077 & 3.34\degree{} \\
10 & 0.50 & 0.368 & 180\degree{} \\
20 & 1.00 & 0.951 & 180\degree{} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The model tolerates conditioning noise up to approximately 5\degree{} of rotation error, producing output that improves upon the conditioning input. At 10\degree{}, the conditioning signal becomes misleading and the model collapses to degenerate solutions ($180\degree{}$ ARE indicates random orientations). The transition is sharp rather than gradual, indicating a well-defined basin boundary. Our self-conditioned prior ($\sim$4.4\degree{} error) lies near the edge of this basin, which explains both why self-refinement succeeds and why it saturates after 2--3 iterations as the estimate approaches the basin's contraction limit.

\subsection{Convergence Behaviour}

Table~\ref{tab:convergence} reports per-iteration metrics for the full hybrid MC method.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Convergence of iterated hybrid MC refinement ($S=4$ samples per iteration). Iteration~2 achieves the minimum ARE; iteration~4 produces an orientation flip in the rotation estimate.}
\label{tab:convergence}
\begin{tabular}{@{}ccccc@{}}
\toprule
Iteration & ATE & ARE (\degree{}) & RPE$_R$ (\degree{}) & RPE$_t$ \\
\midrule
0 (unconditioned) & 0.161 & 4.39 & 6.26 & 0.375 \\
1 & 0.162 & 3.43 & 4.88 & 0.321 \\
2 & 0.160 & 3.13 & 4.58 & 0.309 \\
3 & 0.163 & 3.38 & 4.70 & 0.307 \\
4 & 0.160 & 180\degree{} & 4.78 & 8.55 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The method converges monotonically through iterations 1--2, achieving a 29\% ARE reduction. Iteration~3 shows slight regression, and iteration~4 produces catastrophic failure (orientation flip in at least one camera). This behaviour is consistent with the basin-of-attraction analysis: as the conditioned estimate approaches the $\sim$3\degree{} fixed point, the margin between the estimate's error and the basin boundary narrows, making the iteration sensitive to perturbation noise. We adopt a conservative stopping criterion of $M=2$ iterations or ARE improvement below $0.1\degree{}$ between successive iterations.

\subsection{Inefficacy of Sim(3) Re-alignment}

One might hypothesise that translation degradation under na\"ive iteration is caused by scale drift---each iteration's output uses a different arbitrary scale, and this accumulates. To test this, we Sim(3)-align each iteration's output back to the conditioning frame before re-injection. Table~\ref{tab:sim3} shows that this intervention has no measurable effect.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Sim(3)-constrained vs.\ unconstrained na\"ive iteration. Constraining scale does not prevent translation degradation, confirming that the failure mechanism is systematic conditioning bias rather than scale drift.}
\label{tab:sim3}
\begin{tabular}{@{}ccccc@{}}
\toprule
Iter & Sim(3) ATE & Sim(3) ARE & Unconstrained ATE & Unconstrained ARE \\
\midrule
0 & 0.152 & 4.69\degree{} & 0.152 & 4.69\degree{} \\
1 & 0.160 & 3.50\degree{} & 0.160 & 3.50\degree{} \\
2 & 0.180 & 3.31\degree{} & 0.179 & 3.29\degree{} \\
5 & 0.197 & 3.61\degree{} & 0.195 & 3.59\degree{} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The near-identical degradation trajectories confirm that translation failure is not a scale problem but a structural one: Pi3X's conditioning mechanism introduces systematic translational bias that is independent of the output's global scale. This motivates our decision to anchor translations entirely to the unconditioned source rather than attempting to correct the conditioned output.
