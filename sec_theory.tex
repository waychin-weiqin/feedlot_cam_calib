\section{Theoretical Analysis}
\label{sec:theory}

We provide a formal interpretation of the self-conditioning dynamics observed in Section~\ref{sec:method}, explaining why rotation converges while translation diverges, and connecting the Monte Carlo perturbation strategy to established frameworks.

\subsection{Contraction Mapping on SO(3)}

Let $f: \text{SO}(3)^N \rightarrow \text{SO}(3)^N$ denote the composite mapping from conditioning rotations to Pi3X's output rotations after pipeline processing (gauge fix, outlier rejection, Fréchet mean). We define the geodesic distance on SO(3) as:
\begin{equation}
    d_{\text{SO}(3)}(R_1, R_2) = \|\log(R_1^\top R_2)\|_F
\end{equation}
where $\log: \text{SO}(3) \rightarrow \mathfrak{so}(3)$ is the matrix logarithm. This equals the angle of the relative rotation $R_1^\top R_2$.

If $f$ is a contraction mapping, i.e., there exists $\lambda < 1$ such that for all $R$ in a neighbourhood of the true rotations $R^*$:
\begin{equation}
    d_{\text{SO}(3)}(f(R), R^*) \leq \lambda \cdot d_{\text{SO}(3)}(R, R^*)
\end{equation}
then by the Banach fixed-point theorem, the iteration $R^{(n+1)} = f(R^{(n)})$ converges to a unique fixed point $R^\infty$ satisfying $f(R^\infty) = R^\infty$.

\paragraph{Empirical measurement.} We measure the contraction rate by feeding GT poses corrupted with known noise levels and comparing input error to output error (Section~\ref{sec:ablation}, Table~\ref{tab:noise_robustness}):
\begin{itemize}
    \item Input error 4.4\degree{} $\rightarrow$ output error 3.5\degree{} ($\lambda \approx 0.80$)
    \item Input error 5.0\degree{} $\rightarrow$ output error 3.3\degree{} ($\lambda \approx 0.67$)
    \item Input error 2.0\degree{} $\rightarrow$ output error 1.6\degree{} ($\lambda \approx 0.80$)
\end{itemize}

The contraction rate is approximately $\lambda \approx 0.8$ in the 2--5\degree{} regime. This predicts convergence to a fixed point at:
\begin{equation}
    \text{ARE}^* = \frac{\lambda}{1-\lambda} \cdot \epsilon_{\text{irr}} \approx 4 \cdot \epsilon_{\text{irr}}
    \label{eq:fixed_point}
\end{equation}
where $\epsilon_{\text{irr}}$ is the model's irreducible error (the residual even with perfect conditioning). With $\text{ARE}^* \approx 3.1\degree{}$ observed experimentally, this gives $\epsilon_{\text{irr}} \approx 0.8\degree{}$, consistent with the 1.2\degree{} achieved under perfect GT conditioning (which includes additional pipeline processing noise).

\paragraph{Basin of attraction.} The contraction property holds only within a finite neighbourhood of $R^*$. Our noise robustness experiments (Section~\ref{sec:ablation}) show that conditioning with $>10\degree{}$ error causes catastrophic failure (output error jumps to 180\degree{}). This defines a basin of attraction of approximately $\pm 10\degree{}$ around the true solution---our initial estimate (4.4\degree{} error) lies safely within it.

\subsection{Failure of Contraction for Translation}

Pi3X outputs poses in Sim(3), not SE(3). The translation component $\mathbf{t}^c$ is meaningful only relative to the per-run scale $s$. When fed back as SE(3) conditioning, a scale mismatch accumulates:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{t}^{(n+1)} = s^{(n)} \cdot g(\mathbf{t}^{(n)}, R^{(n)}) + \boldsymbol{\beta}
\end{equation}
where $s^{(n)} \neq 1$ in general and $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ represents systematic bias from the conditioning mechanism. Since the scale factor $s^{(n)}$ varies per iteration and is not corrected, this does not satisfy contraction:
\begin{equation}
    \|\mathbf{t}^{(n+1)} - \mathbf{t}^*\| \not\leq \lambda \|\mathbf{t}^{(n)} - \mathbf{t}^*\|
\end{equation}

This analysis motivates our hybrid fusion strategy: rotation contracts and should be iterated; translation does not and should be anchored to the unconditioned estimate.

\subsection{Monte Carlo Perturbation as Posterior Sampling}

Self-conditioning admits an interpretation through the lens of denoising score matching~\cite{vincent2011}. Under the hypothesis that Pi3X's conditioning mechanism was trained with noisy pose inputs (a common data augmentation for robustness), conditioned inference approximates the posterior mean:
\begin{equation}
    f_\theta(R + \boldsymbol{\epsilon}) \approx \mathbb{E}[R^* \mid R + \boldsymbol{\epsilon}, \text{imgs}]
\end{equation}

By running $S$ structured perturbations $\{R \cdot \exp([\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_s]_\times)\}_{s=1}^S$ and pooling all $S \times K$ observations into a single pipeline pass (with Sim(3) alignment across samples to resolve gauge ambiguity), one approximates the marginal posterior mean $\mathbb{E}[R^* \mid \text{imgs}]$ via Monte Carlo integration:
\begin{equation}
    \bar{R} = \text{Pipeline}\left(\bigcup_{s=1}^S \{f_\theta(R \cdot \exp([\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_s]_\times))\}\right)
\end{equation}
The variance of this estimator decreases as $O(1/\sqrt{S})$ when the posterior has non-trivial spread.

\paragraph{Empirical verdict.} Despite the theoretical motivation, our experiments demonstrate that MC perturbation provides no measurable benefit in this regime (Section~\ref{sec:ablation_S}). We carefully design the sampling procedure to preserve inter-camera geometry (structured perturbation with shared global rotation), resolve per-sample gauge ambiguity (Sim(3) alignment before pooling), and retain cross-sample information (pool-then-pipeline rather than pipeline-per-sample). Even so, the best multi-sample configuration ($S=8$) merely matches single-sample performance. The explanation is that the model's conditional posterior $p(R^* \mid R + \boldsymbol{\epsilon}, \text{imgs})$ is so tightly concentrated (angular standard deviation $<$0.02\degree{} across samples) that the $O(1/\sqrt{S})$ variance reduction operates on a negligible quantity. The conditioning signal places the model in a near-deterministic regime where all perturbations within the basin of attraction converge to the same output. This does not invalidate the posterior sampling interpretation---it reveals that the posterior is unimodal with negligible variance, making deterministic iteration ($S=1$) strictly preferable.

\subsection{Connection to Iterative Denoising}

The overall procedure---starting from a noisy estimate, applying a learned map that moves closer to the truth, and iterating---shares the structure of denoising diffusion~\cite{song2021}:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Start with a noisy estimate $R^{(0)}$ (unconditioned Pi3X output, $\sim$4.4\degree{} error)
    \item Apply a learned denoiser $f_\theta$ conditioned on images
    \item The denoiser contracts the estimate toward the clean manifold
    \item Iterate 2--3 times until convergence
\end{enumerate}

The key differences from standard diffusion are: (i) we start close to the solution rather than from pure noise, requiring only 2--3 steps; (ii) we do not train a noise-level-conditional model; and (iii) convergence is restricted to the rotation subgroup, necessitating the hybrid decoupling. This suggests a broader principle: \emph{pretrained geometry models with conditioning interfaces can serve as implicit denoisers at test time, even without explicit denoising training.}
